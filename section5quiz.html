You've got an experienced group of confident players, but English is not their first language. You notice they're struggling to read the instructions for a puzzle that requires understanding a written component. You try reading it out loud for them, but it's still a struggle... They ask if they can use their phone camera to translate the written instructions.

What should you say?

✓

Correct

Accommodating language needs while staying in character creates an inclusive experience. The player's enjoyment matters more than strict "no phones" rules.

A family group with two kids (ages 8 and 10) is playing. The kids are getting visibly frustrated with a complex puzzle involving multiple steps. The parents are trying to help but the kids are starting to lose interest and one is close to tears.

What's your best approach?

✓

Correct

With young players, intervene earlier if frustration appears. Prioritize encouragement over challenge, and break complex tasks into smaller wins.

A group tells you upfront that they're "escape room enthusiasts" who have completed over 50 rooms. They're 25 minutes in and slightly stuck on a puzzle, but actively discussing theories and seem engaged.

What's your best approach?

✓

Correct

Experienced players often prefer minimal intervention. They're enjoying the challenge — let them lead, and respect their expertise.
