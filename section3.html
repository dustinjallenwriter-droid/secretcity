Questions > Answers


In this lesson, you'll discover how question-based hinting empowers teams to solve puzzles on their own terms. You'll gain practical tools to use questions to guide players toward achieving their own success.


These objectives will guide you through the essential skills for mastering question-based hinting:

  • Understand why questions are more effective than answers
  • Practice using open-ended question stems to prompt player thinking
  • Apply the escalating hint ladder to scaffold problem-solving
  • Identify and avoid common mistakes in hinting

Why Questions Work


When you ask the right questions instead of providing answers:

Questions force players to think through the problem themselves. This builds their problem-solving skills and keeps them mentally engaged with the game.

When players arrive at the solution themselves, they get to celebrate a genuine win. The victory feels earned, not handed to them.

Most players are closer to the solution than they realize. They just need to organize, articulate, and discuss with their team. Questions help them surface that knowledge.

Players interpret question-based hints as support, not instruction. Their answer feels self-generated rather than gifted by the Clue Staff.

Bonus: When you ask instead of tell, you gain insight into what the team actually needs, allowing you to tailor your support and maintain the excitement of the game.

Your Question Toolkit


Explore these categories of questions to guide players without giving away solutions. Each type serves a unique purpose in helping teams move forward.

Use these to gauge where the team is at:

  • "How are we doing over here?"
  • "What are we thinking?"
  • "Do we have any theories?"
  • "What progress have we made?"

Help players take stock of what they know:

  • "What information do we have?"
  • "What information do we need?"
  • "Is there something we're missing?"
  • "What do you think the red letters mean?"

Guide players through their thinking:

  • "Okay, talk me through what we know so far?"
  • "What do we think this is going to lead us to?"
  • "What's our main objective again?"

Draw attention to details without revealing them:

  • "Do we notice anything interesting about this element?"
  • "Does anything stand out about the items?"
  • "Is anything similar between these items? Different or unusual?"

The Escalating Hint Ladder


Hints should always start broad and only become more specific if absolutely necessary. This approach maximizes player satisfaction by giving them every opportunity to solve puzzles independently.

Step 1

Start with Assessment

"How are we doing over here?" — Gauge where they're at before offering anything.

Step 2

Ask About Their Thinking

"What are we thinking?" or "Do we have any theories?" — Let them articulate their current approach.

Step 3

Prompt Information Organization

"What information do we have? What do we still need?" — Help them take stock.

Step 4

Guide Attention

"Do we notice anything interesting about this?" — Draw focus without revealing.

Step 5

Narrow Focus (If Needed)

"What about the red letters specifically?" — Only get specific if broader hints haven't worked.

Putting It Together


Even experienced Clue Staff can sometimes give away too much or too little. It's an honest mistake that happens all the time, and it never feels great to diminish player satisfaction.


Using questions to guide players preserves the challenge and excitement of the escape room. Escalate your hints only as needed, and avoid common pitfalls by focusing on player-driven discovery.


With practice, you'll become a master at supporting teams without ever giving away the solution.
