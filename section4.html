The Magic of Staying in Character


In this multi-faceted role of Clue Staff, hinting belongs to the game facilitation part of the job — but we can't forget that, even when the final seconds are ticking away, this is still immersive theatre.


Filter your hints through a narrative-based lens that furthers, rather than breaks, the game's world. By delivering hints as part of the story, players remain fully engaged in the world you and your colleagues have worked so hard to create.


These objectives will guide you:

  • Understand how story and character shape effective hinting
  • Practice giving hints that preserve immersion
  • Recognize hints that enhance vs. disrupt the story
  • Identify when stepping out of character is necessary

Why Immersion Matters


Hints should feel like seamless moments within the story, not interruptions that remind players they're in a game.


As Clue Staff, your character is woven into the world of the game — your hints should reflect that. When you stay in character, you reinforce the narrative, making players feel like true protagonists on a quest, rather than participants being guided by an outside force.


This approach keeps the magic alive and ensures players remain emotionally invested in their journey.

Principles of Immersive Hinting


Expand each principle to learn how to keep your hints in-world and story-driven.

Maintaining your role, voice, and demeanor ensures that every hint feels authentic to the story. Whether you're a shifty bartender or an eccentric alchemist, your character should be the lens through which all hints are delivered.

Staying in character helps players suspend disbelief and deepens their emotional connection to the experience.

Frame your hints using language and references that belong in the game's world. Instead of giving direct instructions, weave your guidance into the narrative — refer to objects, events, or lore that make sense for your character.

This keeps the illusion intact and makes hints feel like natural story beats.

Always position the players as the protagonists. Your hints should empower them to make discoveries and solve challenges, rather than making your character the source of all answers.

Even though you know the answers, your character depends on the players to figure them out! Frame your guidance as support for their journey.

When to Break Character


While immersion is a top priority, player safety and well-being always come first. Expand each scenario to see the best practice response.

Usually stay in character by weaving the issue into the story.

For example: "The evil alchemist's magic seems to be interfering with our environment — give me a moment to commune with the ancient forces."

Only break character if absolutely necessary.

Break character briefly to support the player.

If someone is visibly upset or anxious, step out of character to offer reassurance and options, then return to the story when appropriate.

Break character immediately if there is any risk to player safety.

Address the situation directly and clearly, prioritizing well-being over immersion.

Do not break character. Use in-character urgency to communicate time limits.

For example: "Markoth's minions are nearly upon us!"

Putting It Together


Narrative-based hinting keeps players immersed and makes every clue feel like part of the adventure.


Delivering hints in character enhances the story, while knowing when to break character ensures player safety and comfort.


Striking this balance is key to creating memorable, engaging escape room experiences.
